A brain is one complete, self-contained memory. Think of it as a single filing cabinet tended by its own librarian: everything inside it is organised, searchable, and connected, but nothing spills over into the cabinet next door. Each brain contains Pages where knowledge lives, an Inbox where new information waits to be filed, a search engine that works on meaning rather than keywords, and a list of Connections: the agents and tools you have approved to access that specific brain.

## What lives inside a brain

**Pages** are the filed knowledge itself. Each one is written as readable text, placed in a sensible folder, linked to related Pages, and finished with a source note at the bottom recording where the information came from and when. Every change is saved in a version history, so nothing is ever quietly lost.

**The Inbox** is the arrival tray. When something new enters a brain, such as a document you dropped in, a note your agent saved, or an item a connected tool sent over, it lands in the Inbox first. Nothing skips this step. The librarian works through the Inbox to produce filed Pages.

**Search** works by meaning. You ask a question in plain words and Cortex finds Pages that answer it, even if none of them contain your exact phrasing. Search is scoped to one brain at a time: asking a question of one brain never surfaces results from another.

**Connections** are the agents and tools you have granted access. Each Connection is approved individually, and each one can be revoked individually. A Connection approved for one brain cannot see that any other brain exists.

## Brains are separate on purpose

Two brains sitting side by side in your account are invisible to each other. An agent connected to one brain cannot read Pages in another, cannot search another, and does not know another brain exists. If you want one agent to work across two brains, you connect it to each one separately and approve each Connection on its own terms.

This separation is what makes it safe to capture sensitive material in your personal brain while sharing a team brain with colleagues.

## Personal and shared brains

There are two kinds of brain, and the difference matters more than anything else in Cortex.

| | Personal brain | Shared brain |
|---|---|---|
| **How it exists** | Created automatically when your account is created | Created deliberately by you or a teammate |
| **Who can see it** | Only you, permanently | The members it is shared with, each by role |
| **How things arrive** | Everything defaults here unless you choose otherwise | Only what someone with Write access deliberately sends |
| **Can it be shared?** | Never | Yes, that is the point of it |
| **Can it be deleted?** | No | Yes, by the brain's Owner |

Your personal brain is the default home for everything you capture. Shared brains are where teams build knowledge together, and things only get into them on purpose.

## What a filed Page looks like

When something arrives in an Inbox and is filed, the result is a Page a human would be happy to read. It has a clear title, sits in a folder that makes sense for the subject, links to related Pages elsewhere in the brain, and ends with a source note stating where the information came from and when. Every edit to a Page is recorded in its version history.

<Note>
The source note at the bottom of a filed Page tells you what the librarian used to create it. It is the trail that lets you check the original if you ever need to.
</Note>

## Try it

1. Open **Brains** and click into your Personal brain.
2. Browse the Pages, then open one and read the source note at the bottom.
3. Use the brain's search box to ask a question in plain words rather than keywords.

---
title: "What is a brain?"
description: "One self-contained memory: pages, inbox, search and connections"
---

A brain is one complete, self-contained memory. Each brain has:

- **Pages**: the filed knowledge itself, written as readable text, organised into folders, linked together, with citations back to sources and full version history.
- **An inbox**: the tray where new information waits to be filed. Nothing skips it.
- **Search**: ask a question of one brain and it searches that brain's pages by meaning, not just keywords.
- **Connections**: the agents and tools you have granted access to this brain, each one individually approved and individually revocable.

Brains are separate on purpose. Searching one brain never returns results from another, and an agent connected to one brain cannot see that any others exist. If you want an agent to use two brains, you connect it to each one separately, and each connection is approved on its own terms.

## Personal and shared

There are two kinds of brain, and the difference matters more than anything else in Cortex:

| | Personal brain | Shared brain |
|---|---|---|
| How it exists | Created with your account | Created deliberately, by you or a teammate |
| Who can see it | Only you, forever | The members it is shared with, by role |
| How things arrive | Everything defaults here | Only what someone deliberately sends |
| Can it be shared? | Never | That is the point of it |
| Can it be deleted? | No | Yes, by an Owner |

Your [personal brain](/brains/your-personal-brain) is the default home for everything you capture. [Shared brains](/brains/shared-brains) are where teams build knowledge together, and things only get into them on purpose.

## What a filed page looks like

When something is filed, the result is a page a human would be happy to read: a short, plain-language write-up with a title, placed in a sensible folder, linked to related pages, and ending with a note of where the information came from and when. Every change to a page is kept in its version history.

## Try it

1. Open **Brains** and click into your Personal brain.
2. Browse its pages, then open one and look for the source note at the bottom.
3. Use the brain's search box to ask a question in plain words rather than keywords.

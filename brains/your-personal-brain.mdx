---
title: "Your personal brain"
description: "Private forever, by design rather than by setting"
---

Every account has exactly one personal brain. Yours was created the moment you joined, it is called Personal, and three things are permanently true about it:

1. **It cannot be shared.** There is no share button to find, no setting to flip, no role to grant. Not "private until you change it": private, full stop.
2. **It cannot be deleted.** It is a structural part of your account.
3. **No one can be invited into it.** Teammates in your workspace can share other brains with you, but nobody, including workspace admins, can see into your personal brain.

## Why so strict?

Because your personal brain is the **default destination** for everything you capture. When your agent saves a conversation, when you drag a file into an inbox without thinking too hard, when a connected tool pushes something in overnight, it all lands in your personal brain unless you deliberately pointed it somewhere else.

A default destination only works if it is completely safe. You should never have to stop and ask "wait, who else will see this?" before capturing a half-formed thought, a sensitive note, or a rambling conversation with your agent. The answer is always: nobody. That is only trustworthy if it can never quietly become untrue, which is why it is built in rather than switched on.

The strictness is the feature. Capture freely; decide what to share later, deliberately.

## Getting things out of it

Knowledge often starts private and earns its way to the team: you capture meeting notes privately, and later the decision inside them belongs in the team brain. The route for that is [promoting](/brains/promoting), a deliberate copy from your personal brain into a shared one. It is the only outbound path, and it never happens on its own.

## Try it

1. Open your Personal brain and look at its settings: notice what is not there. No members, no sharing.
2. Ask your connected agent to *"save a note that I prefer morning meetings"* and watch it arrive in the Personal inbox.
3. Check the filed page afterwards: private, cited, and yours alone.

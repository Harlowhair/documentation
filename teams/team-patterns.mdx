There is no single correct way to organise Cortex for a team. What follows are four patterns that work well in practice. Pick the one that fits how your team already operates, adapt it, and treat it as a starting point rather than a prescription.

## Pattern 1: A team knowledge base

Create one shared Brain, named something like "Team Handbook", using the [Company template](/brains/templates). Add your team as members: most people at Write so everyone can contribute, and a couple of trusted people at Admin to manage the Brain's settings and membership.

Everyone files what they learn into it, either directly or by having their agent do so. Everyone can search and recall from it. Because filing follows [Skills](/filing/skills), the handbook stays consistent regardless of who contributes: the librarian applies the same filing rules whether content comes from one person or twenty.

Start the Brain on Propose so the team stays in control of what lands in the cabinet. Once you trust how items are being filed, move it to Automatic.

<Tip>
  Assign two Admins rather than one from the start. If one person leaves or changes roles, the Brain stays well-managed without any disruption.
</Tip>

## Pattern 2: A client or project Brain

Create a dedicated shared Brain for each client or project. Point relevant [meetings](/capture/meetings-granola) straight at it, add the people working on that account as members, and keep everything about that relationship in one place: contacts, decisions, commitments, and history.

When the project ends, the Brain is a complete, searchable record of everything that happened. Nothing gets lost in email threads or personal notes that leave with a person.

<Note>
  You can archive a Brain once a project closes so it does not clutter your active view, while remaining fully searchable for future reference.
</Note>

## Pattern 3: Meetings into a shared Brain

One person connects [Granola](/capture/meetings-granola) to a shared project Brain. From that point on, the whole team's view of the project's meetings builds up in one place automatically. Others can promote their own relevant meeting notes in from their personal Brains as needed, so the shared Brain accumulates context over time without anyone having to copy things manually.

<Tip>
  This pattern works especially well for client work where continuity matters. A new team member added partway through a project can read back through the meeting history and get up to speed quickly.
</Tip>

## Pattern 4: Private first, shared deliberately

This is the pattern underneath all of the others. People capture freely into their personal Brains without worrying about what is relevant to the team. Then they [promote](/brains/promoting) the parts that genuinely belong to a shared Brain.

Nobody has to self-censor while capturing because their personal Brain is entirely private. Shared Brains contain only what someone actively chose to put there. The result is shared Brains that are focused and useful, not cluttered with every half-formed note anyone ever wrote.

<Info>
  Promotion copies content from your personal Brain to a shared one. Your original stays where it is, private to you, unless you choose to delete it.
</Info>
